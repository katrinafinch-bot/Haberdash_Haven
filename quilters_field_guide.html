✦Welcome

Welcome to your craft. This guide takes you from “I’m not even sure what a fat quarter is” to confidently choosing the right needle, picking the right thread, cutting your fabric cleanly, and setting yourself up to succeed on every project. You do not need years of experience — you need to know the language, the tools, and which supply goes with which job. That’s what this is.

How to use this guide

  • Work through it in order. Each section builds vocabulary you’ll reuse later.
  • Don’t memorize — recognize. Your goal is to know what a thing is for and when you’d reach for it, not to recite specs. The reference tables are there to look things up whenever you need them.
  • Every section ends the same way: a Quick Notes recap and a short Quiz with an answer key. Take the quiz — it’s how you find the gaps.
  • The very end has a one-page Cheat Sheet. Keep it handy at your sewing table.
The one idea to carry through the whole guide

Almost everything in quilting and sewing comes down to matching the right supply to the project. The needle matches the fabric. The thread matches the needle and the job. The batting matches how the quilt will be used. The stabilizer matches the stitch. When you internalize “what goes with what,” your projects go smoothly — and you spend your time creating instead of troubleshooting. That’s the whole idea.

What you’ll be working with

Most of your sewing room touches a handful of categories: fabric (yardage or precuts), thread, batting, needles and machine parts, notions (the huge bucket of small tools — rulers, scissors, pins, markers), and patterns and kits. The chapters ahead walk through each one. Let’s go.

1Sewing Machine Needles

The needle is the cheapest part of the whole operation and the one that causes the most misery when it’s wrong. A machine that seems “broken” very often just has a dull, bent, or wrong needle. Learn this section and you’ll solve problems before they start.

Reading the package: size

Needle sizes are written as two numbers, like 80/12. The first is the European size, the second is American — same needle, two scales. The rule that matters: the bigger the number, the thicker/heavier the needle. Light, delicate fabric wants a small number (60/8, 70/10). Heavy denim or many quilt layers want a big one (90/14, 100/16).

Point types

The shape of the tip decides how the needle enters the fabric. A sharp point pierces tightly woven cloth cleanly. A ballpoint is rounded so it slides between the loops of a knit instead of cutting them (which is what causes runs and holes). A universal sits in between for everyday use.

The needle habits that prevent 80% of problems

Change the needle often — roughly every project, or every 6–8 hours of sewing. They go dull fast.

Flat side of the shank faces the back on almost every home machine.

Skipped stitches, clunking, or breaking thread? Change the needle first. It’s the cheapest fix and usually the right one.

Match the needle to the fabric AND thread

The wrong needle doesn’t just sew poorly — it can put permanent holes in a knit, shred metallic thread, or snap and fly. Once you know your fabric and your thread, you can pick the right needle every time.

Quick Notes
  • Bigger number = thicker needle. Light fabric → small number; heavy fabric → big number.
  • Ballpoint for knits, sharp/Microtex for precise wovens, universal when unsure.
  • Specialty threads need specialty needles (embroidery, metallic, topstitch).
  • Change needles often; a fresh needle fixes most “my machine is broken” complaints.
  • Flat side of the shank usually faces the back.

2Thread

Thread is sold by weight (how thick) and fiber (what it’s made of). Those two facts tell you what a thread is good for. Get comfortable here and you can match thread to almost any project you take on.

Weight: the number that confuses everyone

Thread weight works backwards from what you’d expect: the higher the number, the THINNER the thread. A 50wt is the all-purpose standard for piecing. A 40wt is a touch heavier and standard for machine embroidery and longarm quilting. A 12wt is thick and shows off (big-stitch, topstitching). A 60–80wt is super fine for delicate applique and for the bobbin.

Top thread vs. bobbin thread

They don’t have to match. Many quilters and embroiderers run a fine 60wt thread in the bobbin to reduce bulk and lint. We’ll cover bobbins in their own section — for now, just know it’s normal and not a mistake.

Embroidery thread: the brand question

Machine embroiderers are loyal to brands, and they’ll ask your opinion. The big split is rayon vs. polyester: rayon gives the most luxurious sheen but is weaker and can fade or bleed; polyester is strong, colorfast, and survives bleach — the safer pick for anything that gets washed hard (kids’ clothes, towels, sports gear). Want maximum shine for a decorative piece that won’t be abused? Rayon (Madeira, Sulky). Want it strong and washable for everyday or kids’ items? Polyester (Isacord, Floriani, Glide). When totally unsure, polyester is the lower-risk pick.

  • Cotton: matte, lint, great for quilts. Polyester: strong, slight sheen, washable.
  • Embroidery: rayon = most sheen but fragile; polyester = strong & colorfast.
  • Metallic and monofilament need special handling (metallic needle; low iron heat).
  • Top and bobbin thread don’t have to match.

3Presser Feet

A presser foot is the little attachment that holds fabric down against the feed while you sew. Swapping to the right foot is how you go from “struggling” to “effortless.” You just need to know which one solves which problem.

Two feet that solve the most common quilting headaches

Layers shifting or puckering while quilting? A walking foot feeds all layers evenly — it’s the single best fix for anyone quilting a finished sandwich on a home machine.

Want swirly, free-form quilting? That’s a free-motion foot, and your machine must be able to lower (drop) its feed dogs.

Quick Notes

  • 1/4" foot = piecing. Walking foot = quilting layers. Free-motion = feed dogs down.
  • Open-toe = best view for applique & satin stitch. Zipper foot = zippers, piping.
  • Feet are usually machine-specific — confirm your brand/model before buying one.

4Scissors & Cutting Tools

Cutting tools are sorted by job, not by craft — but quilters, garment sewists, and embroiderers reach for different ones. Knowing the difference lets you pick the tool that actually fits your hands and your project.

ToolUseNote
Dressmaker shearsCutting fabric yardage by handBent handle keeps fabric flat; fabric only — never paper
Embroidery scissorsTrimming threads & tiny detail cutsSmall, very sharp fine points
Thread snipsQuick clip of thread tails at the machineSpring action; one-hand squeeze
Pinking shearsZigzag edge that resists frayingQuick seam finish on wovens

Who reaches for what

  • Quilters mostly cut with a rotary cutter, mat, and ruler — straight strips and precise shapes. They’ll still want snips at the machine and small applique scissors.
  • Garment sewists rely on dressmaker shears for cutting out pattern pieces, plus pinking shears for seam finishes.
  • Embroiderers want small sharp embroidery scissors and curved-tip snips to clip jump threads close to the stitching.
The cardinal rule of fabric scissors

Fabric shears are for fabric only. Cutting paper dulls them instantly, and you’ll be genuinely upset if your good shears get ruined. Many people tie a ribbon on their fabric scissors so no one grabs them for paper.

Rotary cutters are surgically sharp. Always close/retract the blade the moment you set it down, and cut away from the body against the ruler.

Quick Notes
  • Rotary cutter + mat + ruler = the quilter’s main cutting system.
  • Dressmaker shears = garment cutting; embroidery scissors/snips = thread trimming.
  • Duckbill/applique scissors protect the layer underneath.
  • Fabric scissors NEVER cut paper. Close rotary blades every time.

5Fabric: Types & Cutting

Fabric is the heart of the craft, and the cutting mat is where you’ll spend real time. This section gives you the vocabulary, the precut sizes, and the cutting basics so you can identify, measure, and cut accurately.

Width, grain, and selvage

Fabric comes on a bolt about 42–44" wide — that width is called the width of fabric (WOF). The tightly finished edges running down both sides are the selvages. The grain runs parallel to the selvage and is the stable direction; the diagonal bias stretches. To cut, you fold the fabric selvage to selvage so the cut is straight and on grain.

Fat quarters & precuts

A fat quarter is a quarter-yard of fabric cut the “fat” way — about 18" × 22" instead of a skinny 9" × 44" strip. Same amount of fabric, more useful shape. Precuts are bundles the manufacturer already cut into popular sizes so quilters can buy a whole coordinated collection at once.

Buying yardage

You buy yardage in fractions of a yard. The quick map: 9" = 1/4 yard, 18" = 1/2 yard, 27" = 3/4 yard, 36" = 1 yard. You’ll ask for something like “two and a half yards” or “a half yard.” When you’re not sure how much to get, check your pattern — patterns list the yardage required.

The question that saves you a sad return trip

Before you leave the shop, ask yourself: “Do I have enough for the backing and binding too?” Running short on backing fabric mid-project is the classic heartbreak — and dye lots change, so you may not be able to match it later.

Quick Notes
  • Quilting cotton ~44" wide is the staple. Width of fabric = WOF.
  • Fat quarter = 18"×22". Jelly roll = 2.5" strips, charm = 5", layer cake = 10".
  • 9"=1/4 yd, 18"=1/2 yd, 27"=3/4 yd, 36"=1 yd. Yardage is sold in fractions.
  • Fold selvage-to-selvage and cut on grain. Always check you have backing & binding.

6Batting

Batting (also called wadding) is the fluffy middle layer of a quilt — the part that makes it warm and gives it that quilted texture. You choose it by fiber and loft (thickness), and the right pick depends on how the quilt will be used and how you’ll quilt it.

Fiber choices

FiberFeel & resultBest for
CottonLow loft, flat, soft, breathable; crinkles when washedTraditional & modern quilts
PolyesterHigher loft, light, springy, resists shrinkingPuffy quilts, comforters, tied quilts
Cotton/poly 80/20Easy to handle with a little gentle loftThe all-purpose go-to
WoolWarm, light, high loft but breathable; springs backShow quilts, hand quilting
Bamboo & blendsSoft, drapey, eco-friendlyDrapey quilts, garments
SilkVery light, warm, luxurious, low loftSpecial-occasion & garment quilts
FusibleLight adhesive — baste the sandwich with an ironSmall projects; skip pin-basting

Loft, scrim, and “quilting distance”

Loft is thickness: low loft lies flat and modern, high loft is puffy and warm. Scrim is a thin web needled into some battings to add strength and keep fibers from pulling apart — great for machine quilting and durability, slightly less soft. Needlepunched batting is densely needled together (stable, flat); bonded batting is held with a light resin or heat finish on the surface. The single most useful number is on the label: the maximum quilting distance — how far apart the stitching lines can be before the batting shifts or bunches inside the quilt.

That “antique crinkle” look

Cotton batting shrinks a little the first time a quilt is washed, which puckers the fabric slightly and gives that cozy, vintage, crinkled texture many quilters love. Whether to prewash batting is a personal preference — there’s no wrong answer, so it’s simply a matter of the look you want.

Brands you’ll see on the shelf

Common names include Warm & Natural / Warm & White, Quilters Dream, Hobbs, and Pellon. Each makes several fibers and lofts, so always come back to fiber + loft + how it’ll be used rather than brand alone.

Quick Notes
  • Cotton = flat, soft, crinkles. Polyester = puffy, light. 80/20 blend = easy all-purpose.
  • Loft = thickness. Wool & silk are warm but specialty; fusible bastes with an iron.
  • The label’s “max quilting distance” tells you how far apart stitching can be.
  • Cotton shrinks slightly — that’s the loved “crinkle” look. Prewashing is optional.

7Bobbins

The bobbin is the small spool that sits under the needle plate and supplies the bottom thread. Half of all “my machine is acting up” problems trace back to the bobbin — the wrong type, a bad wind, or a threading slip. This is your troubleshooting superpower section.

Classes & types

TypeLooks likeUsed in
Class 15Flat, straight sidesMany modern home machines (drop-in & front-load)
Class 66Domed / curved sidesMany vintage and some current machines
L-styleSmallMany embroidery machines & sergers
M-styleLargeLongarm machines
PrewoundFactory-filled, often sided in cardboard/meshEmbroidery & high-volume sewing
Magnetic-coreLooks normal, has a magnetSome machines — but many forbid them (see warning)
Three bobbin rules that prevent damage

Use the exact class your machine specifies. A bobbin that looks close but is a hair off in size or shape causes skipped stitches, jams, and tension chaos.

Metal and plastic are not interchangeable, even in the same class — the weight difference changes the tension.

Magnetic bobbins can confuse or damage some computerized machines — check your manual before using them.

Winding & inserting

A good bobbin is wound evenly and not overfilled — the thread should go on smoothly through the machine’s tension guide, not piled up loose or bulging past the edges. There are two main loading styles: drop-in (you can see the bobbin through a clear cover and lay the thread in the direction the diagram shows) and front-load (the bobbin goes into a removable case, with the thread pulled through a slot and under a little tension spring).

When something goes wrong

Most stitch problems show up as a tension issue — the top and bottom threads aren’t meeting in the middle of the fabric the way they should. The picture below shows what to look for, and the table after it maps the most common complaints to their fixes.

SymptomLikely causeFix
Bird’s nest under the fabricTop thread not in the tension discs; didn’t hold the tailsRe-thread with the presser foot UP; hold both tails for the first stitches
Bobbin thread showing on topTop tension too tight, or a top-threading slipLower the top tension a little; re-thread the top
Loose loops underneathTop thread not seated in the tension discsRe-thread the top with the foot UP

The bouncer at the door (why “presser foot up” matters)

Think of the top tension discs as a bouncer at a club door. When the presser foot is UP, the bouncer’s arms are open — the thread can slip in between the discs and get “checked in” so the machine can control it. When the foot is DOWN, the discs clamp shut — arms crossed — and if you thread now, the thread never actually gets inside the tension system. It sews with no top control, and you get loops and bird’s nests underneath. So: always thread (and re-thread) with the presser foot UP. That one habit fixes a shocking number of “bobbin” problems that were never the bobbin’s fault.

Quick Notes
  • Match the exact bobbin class; metal ≠ plastic; check before using magnetic.
  • Wind evenly, never overfill. Drop-in vs front-load are the two loading styles.
  • Balanced stitch hides the knot in the middle. Thread on a surface = that side too tight.
  • A bird’s nest underneath is usually a TOP-thread problem, not the bobbin.
  • When in doubt, re-thread BOTH top and bobbin with the presser foot UP.

8Stabilizers & Interfacing

These are the hidden support layers. Stabilizer goes under (or on top of) fabric so machine embroidery and decorative stitches come out crisp instead of puckered. Interfacing adds body and structure to garments and bags. It’s easy to get overwhelmed by the wall of white — two questions narrow it down fast.

Interfacing (for garments & bags)

Interfacing gives shape to the parts that need to hold their form — collars, cuffs, waistbands, bag panels, zipper areas. Two ways it attaches: fusible (iron-on, fast and common) or sew-in (stitched in, for fabrics that can’t take iron heat). It also comes as woven (behaves like fabric, with grain), nonwoven (no grain, economical), and knit (soft, slight stretch — great for knit garments).

The two questions that pick the product

Ask: (1) What’s the fabric? and (2) How permanent does the support need to be? Stretchy knit → cut-away (permanent support). Stable woven → tear-away (easy removal). Sheer or freestanding lace → wash-away. Textured towel or fleece → add a topping on top. Those two questions cut the wall of white down to one or two products.

Quick Notes
  • Stabilizer supports stitching; interfacing shapes garments/bags.
  • Knits & dense designs → cut-away. Stable wovens → tear-away. Sheer/lace → wash-away.
  • A topping goes ON TOP so stitches don’t sink into towels or fleece.
  • Interfacing: fusible (iron-on) vs sew-in; woven / nonwoven / knit.
  • Pick by fabric + how permanent the support must be.

9Notions, Rulers, Mats & Marking Tools

“Notions” is the catch-all for the hundreds of small tools that make sewing possible — the most fun (and most confusing) corner of any sewing room. You won’t memorize all of it; focus on the cutting trio, pins, and marking tools, and you’ll handle the vast majority of questions.

The cutting trio: mat, ruler, rotary cutter

  • Self-healing cutting mat — the soft-but-firm surface that closes back up after each cut. Keep it away from heat and sun, and store it flat — a warped mat is ruined. The printed grid is in inches with helpful angle lines.
  • Acrylic ruler — thick, clear, gridded, with a 45° line for triangles. Common quilting sizes are the 6.5" square and a long 6" × 24" for cutting strips across the fabric.
  • Rotary cutter — a 45mm blade is the standard. Blades dull with use and should be changed often; a dull blade skips and forces dangerous pressure. Retract or close the blade every single time you set it down.

Pins & holders

  • Glass-head pins survive a hot iron (plastic heads melt). Flower-head pins lie flat and are easy to see. Fine applique/silk pins barely mark delicate fabric.
  • Wonder Clips hold thick layers and binding with no holes — a quilter favorite for binding and bag-making.
  • Magnetic pincushion grabs dropped pins fast — but keep it away from computerized machines and magnetic bobbins.

The FriXion warning every quilter should hear

FriXion pens erase with iron heat, which feels magical — but the ink comes back in the cold (a winter car, a freezer) and can leave a faint white “ghost” line that doesn’t fully disappear. For practice and washable projects it’s fine; for heirlooms and show quilts, steer them to a safer marker. And the universal rule: test ANY marker on a fabric scrap first.

  • Seam ripper — everyone’s best friend; for undoing stitches. They dull, so replace yours now and then.
  • Seam gauge, point turner, thimble, needle threader, bias-tape maker, tweezers, fabric glue & basting spray — small helpers; know they exist so you can point to them.
  • Machine needles & bobbins live here too — cross-reference Sections 1 and 7.
Mat & blade care = better cuts

Store your mats flat and out of hot cars (they warp permanently), and change rotary blades at the first sign of skipping. A fresh blade is safer and gives cleaner cuts — keep spares on hand.

Turning a hobby into income changes the math. The skills are the same, but now your time, your costs, and a little record-keeping all matter. This section won’t make you rich overnight — it’ll keep you from accidentally losing money on every quilt you sell.

Custom & commission work

Commissions are where the money often is — and where things go wrong without clear agreements up front.

  • Take a deposit. Materials are bought before you sew; a 50% deposit protects you if the buyer disappears.
  • Write the specs down. Size, colors, style, due date — in a message both of you can see. “I thought it’d be bigger” is a conversation you want to have before cutting.
  • Memory quilts deserve extra care. Clothing from a loved one is irreplaceable. Confirm exactly which garments can be cut, and be honest about timelines — these are emotional, not transactional.
  • It’s okay to say no. A rush job, an unrealistic budget, or a fabric you can’t source is a fair decline. Saying no protects the work you do say yes to.

The money & legal basics

Selling, even occasionally, can cross from hobby into business, and that affects taxes and sales-tax collection. Keep your receipts and a simple record of what sold for how much from day one — far easier than reconstructing it later.

This part isn’t one-size-fits-all

Sales tax, hobby-vs-business rules, and what you can deduct all vary by state and by your situation. Treat this section as orientation, not advice — for anything that affects your taxes or legal setup, check your state’s rules and talk to a CPA. A short conversation early is cheap insurance.

A quick sanity check before you name a price

Add your real materials, add (hours × a rate you’d accept), then ask: “Would I make this again for that price?” If the answer is no, the price is too low — not the quilt too expensive.

1Agujas de Máquina de Coser

La aguja es la pieza más barata de todo el equipo y la que más problemas causa cuando está mal. Una máquina que parece “rota” muchas veces solo tiene una aguja gastada, doblada o equivocada. Aprende esta sección y resolverás problemas antes de que ocurran.

Leer el paquete: el tamaño

Los tamaños se escriben con dos números, como 80/12. El primero es la medida europea, el segundo la americana — misma aguja, dos escalas. La regla clave: a mayor número, aguja más gruesa. La tela fina y delicada pide número pequeño (60/8, 70/10). El dril grueso o muchas capas piden uno grande (90/14, 100/16).

Tipos de punta

La forma de la punta decide cómo entra la aguja en la tela. Una punta fina perfora limpiamente los tejidos cerrados. Una punta de bola es redondeada para deslizarse entre las mallas del género de punto en vez de cortarlas (lo que provoca carreras y agujeros). Una universal queda en medio para el uso diario.

Cambia la aguja con frecuencia — más o menos cada proyecto, o cada 6–8 horas de costura. Se desafilan rápido.

La aguja equivocada no solo cose mal: puede dejar agujeros permanentes en el punto, deshilachar el hilo metálico o romperse y salir disparada. Una vez que sabes tu tela y tu hilo, puedes elegir la aguja correcta siempre.

  • Los hilos especiales necesitan agujas especiales (bordado, metálica, pespunte).
  • Cambia la aguja seguido; una aguja nueva resuelve casi todas las quejas de “se rompió la máquina”.
  • El lado plano del vástago suele ir hacia atrás.

2Hilo

El hilo se vende por grosor (qué tan grueso) y por fibra (de qué está hecho). Esos dos datos te dicen para qué sirve. Domínalo y podrás emparejar hilo con casi cualquier proyecto que emprendas.

Grosor: el número que confunde a todos

El grosor del hilo funciona al revés de lo esperado: a mayor número, hilo MÁS FINO. El 50 es el estándar de uso general para ensamblar. El 40 es algo más grueso, estándar para bordado a máquina y acolchado longarm. El 12 es grueso y luce (puntada grande, pespunte). El 60–80 es muy fino para aplicación delicada y para la bobina.

  • Los bordadores quieren tijeras de bordado pequeñas y corta-hilos de punta curva para cortar al ras los saltos de hilo.
La regla de oro de las tijeras de tela

Las tijeras de tela son solo para tela. Cortar papel las desafila al instante, y te molestarás de verdad si se arruinan tus buenas tijeras. Mucha gente les ata una cinta para que nadie las use con papel.

Los cortadores rotativos están muy afilados. Cierra/retrae la hoja apenas lo sueltes y corta alejándote del cuerpo, contra la regla.

Notas rápidas
  • Cortador rotativo + base + regla = el sistema de corte del quilter.
  • Tijeras de modista = cortar prendas; tijeras de bordado/corta-hilos = cortar hilos.
  • Las tijeras de pico de pato protegen la capa de abajo.
  • Las tijeras de tela NUNCA cortan papel. Cierra el cortador rotativo siempre.

La tela viene en un rollo de unos 42–44" de ancho — ese ancho se llama ancho de tela (WOF). Los bordes acabados a los lados son los orillos. Loft, scrim y “distancia de acolchado”

El loft es el grosor: el loft bajo queda plano y moderno, el alto queda esponjoso y abrigado. El scrim es una malla fina insertada con aguja en algunas guatas para darles resistencia y evitar que las fibras se separen — ideal para acolchar a máquina y para la durabilidad, aunque algo menos suave. La guata needlepunched (apuntada) está densamente unida con agujas (estable, plana); la guata bonded (ligada) se sujeta con una resina o calor ligero en la superficie. El número más útil está en la etiqueta: la distancia máxima de acolchado — cuán separadas pueden ir las líneas de costura antes de que la guata se mueva o se amontone dentro del quilt.

Ese aspecto “arrugado antiguo”

La guata de algodón encoge un poco la primera vez que se lava el quilt, lo que frunce ligeramente la tela y da esa textura acogedora, vintage y arrugada que aman muchos quilters. Prelavar o no la guata es una preferencia personal — no hay respuesta incorrecta, así que es solo cuestión del aspecto que quieras.

7Bobinas (Canillas)

La bobina (o canilla) es el pequeño carrete que va bajo la placa de la aguja y suministra el hilo inferior. La mitad de los problemas de “mi máquina falla” vienen de la bobina — tipo equivocado, mal enrollado o un desliz al enhebrar. Esta es tu sección de superpoderes para resolver problemas.

Tres reglas de bobina que evitan daños

Usa la clase exacta que indica tu máquina. Una bobina parecida pero un pelo distinta en tamaño o forma causa puntadas saltadas, atascos y caos de tensión.

Metal y plástico no son intercambiables, ni en la misma clase — la diferencia de peso cambia la tensión.

Las bobinas magnéticas pueden confundir o dañar algunas máquinas computarizadas — revisa el manual antes de usarlas.

Enrollar e insertar

Una buena bobina se enrolla pareja y sin sobrellenar — el hilo debe entrar suave por la guía de tensión de la máquina, no amontonado ni sobresaliendo de los bordes. Hay dos formas de cargarla: de inserción (drop-in) — ves la bobina por una tapa transparente y colocas el hilo en la dirección del diagrama — y de carga frontal (front-load) — la bobina va en un portabobinas extraíble, con el hilo pasado por una ranura y bajo un pequeño resorte de tensión.

Cuando algo sale mal

La mayoría de los problemas de puntada aparecen como un problema de tensión — los hilos superior e inferior no se encuentran en el medio de la tela como deberían. La imagen de abajo muestra qué buscar, y la tabla siguiente relaciona las quejas más comunes con su solución.

El portero en la puerta (por qué importa “prensatelas arriba”)

Imagina los discos de tensión superior como un portero en la puerta de un club. Con el prensatelas ARRIBA, el portero tiene los brazos abiertos — el hilo se cuela entre los discos y queda “registrado” para que la máquina lo controle. Con el prensatelas ABAJO, los discos se cierran — brazos cruzados — y si enhebras así, el hilo nunca entra al sistema de tensión. Cose sin control arriba, y salen bucles y nidos de pájaro por debajo. Así que: enhebra (y reenhebra) siempre con el prensatelas ARRIBA. Ese solo hábito resuelve muchísimos problemas de “bobina” que nunca fueron culpa de la bobina.

  • La puntada equilibrada esconde el nudo en el medio. Hilo en una superficie = ese lado muy tenso.
  • Un nido de pájaro debajo suele ser problema del hilo SUPERIOR, no de la bobina.
  • En la duda, reenhebra arriba Y la bobina con el prensatelas LEVANTADO.

Entretela (para prendas y bolsos)

La entretela da forma a las partes que deben mantenerla — cuellos, puños, pretinas, paneles de bolsos, zonas de cremallera. Se fija de dos formas: termoadhesiva (se plancha, rápida y común) o de coser (se cose, para telas que no toleran el calor de la plancha). Pregunta: (1) ¿Qué tela es? y (2) ¿Cuán permanente debe ser el soporte? Punto elástico → de recortar (soporte permanente). Tejido plano estable → de rasgar (fácil de quitar). Transparente o encaje independiente → soluble. Toalla con textura o polar → añade una cubierta encima. Esas dos preguntas reducen la pared de opciones a uno o dos productos.

9Notions, Reglas, Bases y Marcadores

“Notions” es el término general para los cientos de herramientas pequeñas que hacen posible coser — el rincón más divertido (y más confuso) de cualquier cuarto de costura. No memorizarás todo; centéstrate en el trío de corte, los alfileres y los marcadores, y resolverás la gran mayoría de las preguntas.

El trío de corte: base, regla, cortador rotativo

  • Base de corte autocicatrizante — la superficie firme pero blanda que se “cierra” tras cada corte. Mantenla lejos del calor y el sol, y guárdala plana — una base alabeada se arruina. La cuadrícula impresa está en pulgadas con líneas de ángulo útiles.
  • Regla acrílica — gruesa, transparente, con cuadrícula y una línea de 45° para triángulos. Tamaños comunes: el cuadrado de 6.5" y una larga de 6" × 24" para cortar tiras a lo ancho.
  • Cortador rotativo — la hoja de 45mm es la estándar. Las hojas se desafilan y deben cambiarse seguido; una hoja gastada salta y obliga a presionar peligrosamente. Retrae o cierra la hoja cada vez que lo sueltes.

Alfileres y sujetadores

  • Los alfileres de cabeza de vidrio aguantan la plancha caliente (las cabezas de plástico se derriten). Los de cabeza de flor quedan planos y se ven fácil. Los finos de aplicación/seda apenas marcan la tela delicada.
  • Los Wonder Clips sujetan capas gruesas y ribetes sin hacer agujeros — favoritos para ribetear y hacer bolsos.
  • El alfiletero magnético recoge alfileres caídos rápido — pero mantenlo lejos de máquinas computarizadas y bobinas magnéticas.

Trabajo por encargo (commission)

Los encargos suelen ser donde está el dinero — y donde las cosas salen mal sin acuerdos claros desde el inicio.

  • Pide un anticipo. Los materiales se compran antes de coser; un 50% de anticipo te protege si el comprador desaparece.
  • Pon las especificaciones por escrito. Tamaño, colores, estilo, fecha — en un mensaje que ambos puedan ver. “Lo creía más grande” es una conversación que quieres tener antes de cortar.
  • Los quilts de recuerdo merecen cuidado extra. La ropa de un ser querido es irremplazable. Confirma exactamente qué prendas pueden cortarse y sé honesta con los plazos — son emocionales, no transaccionales.
  • Está bien decir que no. Una prisa imposible, un presupuesto irreal o una tela que no consigues son un “no” justo. Decir que no protege el trabajo al que sí dices que sí.

Lo básico de dinero y legal

Vender, aunque sea de vez en cuando, puede pasar de pasatiempo a negocio, y eso afecta los impuestos y el cobro de impuesto sobre ventas. Guarda tus recibos y un registro simple de qué vendiste y a cuánto desde el primer día — mucho más fácil que reconstruirlo luego.

Esta parte no es igual para todos

El impuesto sobre ventas, las reglas de pasatiempo-vs-negocio y lo que puedes deducir varían por estado y por tu situación. Toma esta sección como orientación, no como asesoría — para cualquier cosa que afecte tus impuestos o tu estructura legal, revisa las reglas de tu estado y habla con un contador (CPA). Una charla corta al inicio es un seguro barato.

Una verificación rápida antes de dar un precio

Suma tus materiales reales, suma (horas × una tarifa que aceptarías), y luego pregúntate: “¿Volvería a hacer esto por ese precio?” Si la respuesta es no, el precio es muy bajo — no el quilt muy caro.

Notas rápidas
  • Tres métodos: materiales + por hora, por pulgada cuadrada, o materiales × 2–3.
  • Incluye SIEMPRE tu TIEMPO — cobrar solo materiales es el clásico que pierde dinero.
  • Costéalo completo: top + trasera + ribete + guata + hilo + patrón + longarm + horas + comisiones.
  • Vende en ferias, consignación, Etsy, redes o tu propio sitio — cada uno cambia alcance por comisiones/esfuerzo.
  • Encargos: anticipo, especificaciones por escrito, cuidado extra con quilts de recuerdo, y está bien decir que no.
  • Pasatiempo-vs-negocio e impuestos varían — lleva registros y consulta tu estado / un CPA.
